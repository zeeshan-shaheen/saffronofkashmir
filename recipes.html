Beverage — Traditional Kashmiri

Kesar Doodh

Saffron Milk — the golden elixir of Kashmir

Prep20 min
Cook10 min
Total30 min
Serves1–2
Saffron5–6 threads
"Kesar Doodh is not merely a drink — it is a Kashmiri ritual. Prepared before sleep, shared at celebrations, gifted to new mothers. Always made with Mongra."

Ingredients

Base Recipe

  • 1 cup full-fat milk (or plant-based alternative)
  • 5–6 strands Kashmiri Mongra saffron
  • 1–2 teaspoons sweetener (raw honey, jaggery, or cane sugar)
  • 2 tablespoons warm milk (for blooming the saffron)

Traditional Additions (optional)

  • 2 green cardamom pods, lightly crushed
  • A pinch of freshly grated nutmeg
  • 4–5 raw almonds, blanched and slivered
  • 1 teaspoon rose water

Method

1

Bloom the Saffron

Place 5–6 saffron threads in a small bowl with 2 tablespoons of warm (not boiling) milk. Gently crush the threads with the back of a spoon to help release their crocin. Allow to steep for 15–20 minutes — the liquid will turn a deep, clear gold. This step is non-negotiable: blooming unlocks both colour and aroma.

2

Warm the Milk

Pour the remaining milk into a small saucepan. Add crushed cardamom pods if using. Heat over medium-low flame until the milk reaches a gentle simmer — small bubbles at the edges, not a rolling boil. Stir frequently to prevent a skin forming.

3

Combine and Simmer

Pour the bloomed saffron liquid — threads and all — into the simmering milk. Stir gently. Reduce heat to low and simmer for 5–7 minutes, stirring occasionally. The milk will deepen to a warm amber-gold. The longer you simmer, the more intense the colour and flavour.

4

Sweeten and Serve

Remove from heat. Add your sweetener of choice and stir until dissolved. Strain if you prefer a clean cup, or serve with the threads — they are entirely edible. Garnish with almond slivers and a dusting of nutmeg. Serve immediately, or cool and refrigerate (keeps 2 days; reheat gently).

Seven Variations

Traditional Kesar Doodh

Cardamom, a pinch of nutmeg, blanched almonds. The classic Kashmiri recipe — rich, warming, deeply fragrant.

Golden Turmeric Saffron

Add ¼ tsp turmeric, a sliver of ginger, a crack of black pepper. An immunity-focused golden milk for winter mornings.

Rose Saffron Milk

1 tsp rose water, cardamom, a few dried rose petals. Delicate, floral — a beauty-focused variation popular in Mughal court cuisine.

Almond Saffron Milk

Swap dairy for almond milk. Add almond butter for richness. Plant-based but still authentically golden.

Spiced Winter Warmer

Add a cinnamon stick, 2 cloves, and a sliver of ginger alongside the saffron. Strain and serve with jaggery — best drunk after dinner in December.

Coconut Saffron Milk

Full-fat coconut milk in place of dairy. Rich and tropical — works both warm and chilled over ice.

Chocolate Saffron

1 tsp raw cacao, a pinch of cinnamon, saffron bloom. The unexpected pairing that works — bitter chocolate grounds the floral threads.

Bedtime Sleep Blend

Saffron, warm full-fat milk, raw honey, a pinch of nutmeg. Consume 30–45 minutes before sleep. Safranal in the threads promotes calm and restful sleep onset.

Main Course — North Indian

Saffron Paneer Butter Masala

Creamy cottage cheese curry elevated with Kashmiri Mongra

Prep10 min
Bloom10 min
Cook30 min
Total50 min
Serves4–6
"The saffron does not overwhelm the butter masala — it dignifies it. The gravy shifts from orange to amber-gold, and the aroma becomes unmistakable."

Ingredients

Saffron Infusion

  • 15–20 strands Kashmiri Mongra saffron
  • 3 tablespoons warm milk or cream

Paneer

  • 400g paneer, cut into 1-inch cubes
  • 2 tablespoons ghee or butter (for optional frying)
  • Warm salted water (for soaking, keeps paneer soft)

Tomato-Cashew Gravy Base

  • 500g ripe tomatoes, roughly chopped
  • 15–20 cashew nuts
  • 1 large onion (200g), roughly chopped
  • 6–8 garlic cloves
  • 1-inch ginger piece
  • 2 green cardamom pods
  • 2–3 cloves
  • 1-inch cinnamon stick
  • 1 bay leaf

Tempering & Spices

  • 3 tablespoons butter, divided
  • 1 teaspoon cumin seeds
  • 1 teaspoon Kashmiri red chilli powder
  • ½ teaspoon garam masala
  • 1 teaspoon coriander powder
  • ½ teaspoon turmeric powder
  • 1 teaspoon sugar
  • Salt to taste
  • ½ cup fresh cream (minimum 25% fat)
  • 2 tablespoons kasuri methi (dried fenugreek), crushed

Garnish

  • Fresh coriander leaves, chopped
  • 1–2 tablespoons additional cream
  • Extra saffron strands
  • Ginger julienne (optional)

Method

1

Bloom the Saffron (10 minutes)

Place saffron strands in a small bowl with warm milk or cream. Gently crush threads with a spoon. Steep for at least 10 minutes until the liquid turns deep golden-orange. This is your flavour concentrate — do not rush it.

2

Prepare the Paneer (10 minutes)

Cut paneer into 1-inch cubes. For restaurant texture, heat ghee in a pan and lightly fry cubes until golden on each side — 2–3 minutes total. Transfer immediately to warm salted water to keep the interior soft while the exterior firms up.

3

Build the Tomato-Cashew Purée (15 minutes)

Heat 1 tablespoon butter in a deep pan. Add bay leaf, cardamom, cloves, and cinnamon — sizzle 30 seconds. Add onions, sauté 3–4 minutes until translucent. Add ginger and garlic for 2 minutes. Add cashews for 1 minute. Add tomatoes with a pinch of salt; cook covered 8–10 minutes until fully soft. Cool 10 minutes. Remove whole spices. Blend into a smooth purée and strain through a fine sieve for a restaurant-quality finish.

4

Temper the Spices (2 minutes)

Heat 2 tablespoons butter over medium heat. Add cumin seeds — let them crackle for 15–20 seconds. Pour the tomato-cashew purée into the pan. Add chilli powder, coriander powder, turmeric, and salt. Mix well. Cook 5–6 minutes until the oil begins to separate at the edges of the gravy.

5

Add the Saffron Bloom (1 minute)

Pour the bloomed saffron — with all its liquid — into the gravy. Stir gently to distribute the colour evenly. Add 1 teaspoon sugar to balance the tomato acidity. The gravy should shift from orange to a deep amber-gold.

6

Cream and Paneer (3–4 minutes)

Lower heat to medium-low. Add cream and stir gently. If too thick, add ¼–½ cup water. Drain paneer from the salted water and add to the gravy. Simmer 2–3 minutes only — avoid vigorous stirring to prevent the paneer from breaking.

7

Finish and Serve

Crush kasuri methi between your palms and add along with garam masala. Simmer 1 final minute. Optionally stir in another tablespoon of butter for extra richness. Transfer to a serving dish. Drizzle with cream, scatter coriander, ginger julienne, and a few extra saffron threads. Serve with butter naan, roti, or steamed basmati.

Chef's Notes

Always strain the tomato-cashew purée — this single step transforms home cooking into restaurant-quality texture.

Do not overcook the paneer. Two to three minutes in the gravy is sufficient; longer and the cubes become rubbery.

Bloom the saffron in cream rather than milk for a richer, more intense colour yield in the final gravy.

Always crush kasuri methi between your palms before adding — this releases the volatile oils that give the dish its signature finish.

Cook the tomatoes completely before blending. Under-cooked tomatoes produce a raw, acidic gravy that no amount of cream will fix.

Kashmiri red chilli powder is key — it gives deep colour without excessive heat, complementing rather than overpowering the saffron.

Rice — Kashmiri Ceremonial

Zafrani Pulao

Kashmiri Saffron Rice — the gold of the wazwan table

Prep15 min
Soak30 min
Cook25 min
Total1 hr
Serves4
"In every Kashmiri wazwan feast, the Zafrani Pulao arrives last — a fragrant, jewelled rice that signals the meal has reached its peak. The saffron is not decoration. It is the soul of the dish."

Ingredients

Saffron Bloom

  • 20–25 strands Kashmiri Mongra saffron
  • 4 tablespoons warm milk

Rice & Base

  • 2 cups aged Basmati rice, soaked 30 minutes
  • 3 tablespoons ghee
  • 1 large onion, thinly sliced
  • 3½ cups water or light vegetable stock
  • Salt to taste

Whole Spices

  • 2 bay leaves
  • 4 green cardamom pods, lightly cracked
  • 4 cloves
  • 1-inch cinnamon stick
  • 1 black cardamom pod
  • 1 small star anise

Garnish (Mezban Style)

  • 2 tablespoons ghee (finishing)
  • 12–15 raw cashews
  • 2 tablespoons raisins (kishmish)
  • 1 tablespoon blanched almonds, slivered
  • Dried rose petals (optional)
  • Extra saffron strands

Method

1

Bloom the Saffron

Add saffron threads to warm milk. Crush lightly with a spoon. Set aside to steep for 20–30 minutes. The liquid should become a deep, clear saffron gold — this is what will marble through the rice.

2

Fry the Onions

Heat 3 tablespoons ghee in a heavy-bottomed pot over medium-high heat. Add sliced onions with a pinch of salt and fry, stirring regularly, until they turn deep golden-brown — 10–12 minutes. Do not rush this step. The caramelised onions give the pulao its depth. Remove half for garnish; leave half in the pot.

3

Bloom the Whole Spices

Reduce heat to medium. Add all whole spices to the pot with the remaining onions. Sizzle for 30–45 seconds until fragrant. The ghee should perfume the kitchen.

4

Add Rice and Water

Drain the soaked rice and add to the pot. Stir gently for 1 minute, coating each grain in ghee. Add water or stock and salt. Bring to a boil over high heat. Once boiling, reduce to the lowest possible heat, cover tightly, and cook for 12 minutes.

5

Add the Saffron Bloom

After 12 minutes, uncover briefly. Drizzle the saffron milk across the surface of the rice — do not stir. Replace the lid. Continue cooking on the lowest heat for a further 8–10 minutes, or until the rice is fully cooked and the liquid absorbed. The saffron will marble naturally downward.

6

Dum Rest and Garnish

Remove from heat. Leave covered — undisturbed — for 10 minutes. This dum rest allows the steam to finish the rice and the saffron to fully penetrate. Meanwhile, heat 2 tablespoons ghee in a small pan. Fry cashews until golden, add raisins (they puff immediately), then almonds. Uncover the pulao, gently fluff with a fork, and turn out onto a serving platter. Scatter the fried nuts, reserved golden onions, rose petals, and fresh saffron strands.

Chef's Notes

Use aged Basmati — at least one year old. Freshly harvested Basmati has higher moisture content and will turn sticky. Look for "Extra Long" or "Aged" on the packaging.

Soaking the rice for 30 minutes is not optional for Pulao. It relaxes the starch and ensures each grain cooks separately, never clumping.

Do not stir after adding the saffron. The two-tone effect — ivory grains marbled with gold — is achieved only if you let the saffron seep through undisturbed.

The dum rest is as important as the cook. Remove from heat with the lid on and wait the full 10 minutes. Opening early lets the steam escape and the texture suffers.

More saffron is better in Zafrani Pulao. Unlike many recipes where one pinch suffices, this dish is defined by the saffron. Use 20–25 threads, not 5–6.

To serve the traditional wazwan way, layer the pulao on a large copper thaal with the garnishes mounded in the centre. Accompany with Rogan Josh and yoghurt.
