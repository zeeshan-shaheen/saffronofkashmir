What is Mongra Grade Saffron and Why It Matters

Walk into any spice market from Dubai to Delhi and you'll find saffron at prices that range from AED 10 to AED 300 per gram. The reason for this extraordinary range lies in a single word: grade.

Kashmiri saffron is classified into three grades based on which part of the crocus stigma is used. Mongra — the highest — consists entirely of the deep-red stigma tips, separated from the yellow style. Below that is Lachha, which includes the red part plus some yellow style, and Zarda or Gucchi, which includes the entire stigma with the full yellow portion. Each step down in grade means less crocin (the colourant), less picrocrocin (the bitter principle), and less safranal (the aroma compound).

Mongra saffron gives 20–30% more colour per gram than Lachha grade. In a Zafrani Pulao or a pot of Kahwa, the difference is immediately visible — a rich golden hue versus a pale yellow tint. In terms of aroma, Mongra releases a deep, honeyed metallic scent that lingers long after cooking. Lower grades often smell grassy or have almost no distinguishable scent at all.

The labour involved in producing Mongra is extreme. Each saffron crocus produces just 3 stigmas. After hand-harvesting the flowers at dawn — the only time they're open — workers must separate the three red filaments from the yellow style by hand, one flower at a time. For one kilogram of Mongra saffron, approximately 150,000 flowers must be processed. A skilled worker can separate around 1,500 flowers per hour, meaning the labour for one kilogram of Mongra represents 100+ hours of work.

When you buy Mongra grade, you're not just buying saffron — you're buying the most concentrated, potent form of one of the world's most ancient spices. At Saffron of Kashmir, we source exclusively Mongra grade, tested and certified to ISO 3632 Grade A standards.

The Saffron Fields of Pampore: A 700-Year Legacy

Twenty kilometres south of Srinagar, just off the Jammu–Srinagar highway, lies Pampore — a town whose identity is inseparable from the purple bloom of Crocus sativus. Locals call it the "Saffron Town of India," and with good reason: the Karewa plateau on which Pampore sits produces 90% of India's total saffron output, and some of the finest saffron on earth.

The history of saffron cultivation in Kashmir stretches back to at least the 14th century, when Persian traders are believed to have introduced the crocus to the Valley. The Mughal emperors, who spent their summers in Kashmir's gardens, were among the most ardent patrons of Kashmiri saffron — Akbar reportedly ordered saffron to be planted in royal gardens across the subcontinent, but only the Pampore variety produced the legendary quality he sought. The reason lies in the soil: the Karewa formation is a rare geological deposit of glacial silt, high in minerals, with perfect drainage properties for the moisture-sensitive crocus.

The harvest happens in the most dramatic window imaginable: a 15-to-20-day period in October and November, when the purple flowers emerge almost overnight. Entire farming families — women and men alike — move into the fields before dawn, picking flowers by hand before the sun opens them fully. Delay even by an hour, and the petals begin to wilt and the stigmas lose potency. It is one of the most time-pressured agricultural harvests in the world.

By the early 2000s, however, the Pampore saffron industry was in crisis. Declining groundwater levels, erratic snowfall due to climate change, and competition from cheaper Iranian saffron (which, while excellent, is a different variety) pushed many farmers to abandon the crop. The area under saffron cultivation fell from 5,700 hectares in the 1990s to under 2,500 hectares by 2010.

Saffron of Kashmir was founded in 2004 precisely to address this gap — establishing direct partnerships with Pampore farming families at fair prices, ensuring they had the economic incentive to continue growing what the Mughals prized above all spices. Every tin of our saffron represents a direct line from these ancient fields to your kitchen.

How to Test Saffron Purity at Home (3 Methods)

A 2019 study in the Journal of Food Science found that over 50% of commercially available saffron in the Middle East contained adulterants — dyed corn silk, safflower petals, or even coloured paper fibres. If you've ever bought saffron that didn't colour your rice properly or lacked any real aroma, you were likely using adulterated product. Here are three methods to verify authenticity at home.

Method 1: The Cold Water Soak

Place 3–4 threads in a glass of cold water. Observe over 10–15 minutes. Real saffron will slowly release a deep golden-yellow colour. The thread itself will remain crimson — it doesn't turn white or transparent. Fake saffron (dyed threads) bleaches almost immediately and turns pale or colourless, while the water turns a harsh orange-red.

Method 2: The Baking Soda Test

Add a pinch of baking soda to your saffron water. Real saffron turns the water to a bright golden-yellow colour. Adulterated saffron with artificial dye often turns the water red, green, or a murky brown — as the alkaline baking soda reacts with synthetic pigments.

Method 3: The Rub Test

Place a single thread between your fingers and rub gently. Genuine saffron stains your fingers golden-yellow and the thread itself softens but doesn't crumble or disintegrate. Fake dyed threads tend to bleed colour quickly and crumble, while the dye on your fingers often has an unnaturally bright orange-red hue rather than yellow.

Note: These are consumer tests. Professional testing uses HPLC spectrophotometry to measure crocin concentration and ISO 3632 compliance. All our saffron is batch-tested to Grade A standards — certificate available on request.

Cooking with Saffron

Using Kashmiri Saffron in Arabic Cuisine

Saffron has been part of Arab cuisine for over a thousand years, carried along the trade routes from Persia and Kashmir to the Gulf, the Levant, and North Africa. In UAE kitchens today, it appears in everything from the celebratory Machboos to the restorative Harees, from saffron-scented Gahwa to the delicate Umm Ali dessert.

Machboos (Kabsa): The Gulf's answer to biryani. Use 8–10 threads of Mongra saffron, bloomed for 20 minutes in 3 tablespoons of warm (not boiling) water, then added to the rice along with rose water in the final steaming stage. The goal is a subtle golden colour and a floral perfume — not an aggressive yellow.

Saffron Gahwa: Traditional Arabic cardamom coffee is sometimes made with a pinch of saffron added directly to the coffee grounds. Use just 3–4 threads per pot — saffron here is a supporting note, adding warmth and a faint bittersweet edge to the cardamom.

Saffron Milk (Haleeb Zafaran): A warming bedtime drink. Bloom 8–10 threads in a tablespoon of warm milk, then add to your full cup. Add honey, a pinch of cardamom, and a few pistachios. This is the Arabic equivalent of the South Asian Kesar Doodh and is drunk daily across Emirati households.

The Golden Rule: Always bloom your saffron in warm liquid (water, milk, stock — never oil) before adding it to your dish. This releases the crocin pigment and allows even distribution of colour and flavour. Never grind Mongra saffron dry — it destroys the delicate aromatic compounds.

Health Benefits of Saffron Backed by Research

Saffron has been used medicinally for over 3,000 years — in Persian, Ayurvedic, and Unani medical traditions. Modern research has begun to catch up, identifying specific bioactive compounds responsible for many of these traditional uses.

Mood and Mental Health

A 2014 meta-analysis in Human Psychopharmacology reviewed five randomised controlled trials and found saffron supplementation (30mg/day) to be significantly more effective than placebo for mild-to-moderate depression, with an effect comparable to imipramine and fluoxetine. The active compound responsible appears to be crocin, which inhibits reuptake of serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine — the same mechanism as many conventional antidepressants.

Antioxidant Properties

Saffron contains more than 150 volatile and non-volatile compounds, including crocin, crocetin, and safranal. These are among the most powerful plant antioxidants identified, with the ability to neutralise reactive oxygen species and reduce lipid peroxidation — contributing to cardiovascular protection and reduced inflammation.

Vision and Eye Health

A series of studies from the University of L'Aquila (Italy) found that daily saffron supplementation significantly improved vision in patients with age-related macular degeneration (AMD) — the leading cause of vision loss in adults over 50. The crocetin in saffron appears to improve oxygen transport to the retina and protect photoreceptor cells.

Dosage Note

The doses used in most research studies range from 20–100mg of saffron per day. A generous pinch of Mongra saffron (approximately 30mg) in your daily Kahwa or Kesar Doodh falls within the therapeutic range for these benefits. This is not medical advice — consult your healthcare provider for therapeutic applications.
