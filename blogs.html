What is Mongra Grade Saffron and Why It Matters

Walk into any spice market from Dubai to Delhi and you'll find saffron priced from AED 10 to AED 300 per gram. The reason lies in a single word: grade.

Read full article

Kashmiri saffron is classified into three grades based on which part of the crocus stigma is used. Mongra — the highest — consists entirely of the deep-red stigma tips, separated from the yellow style. Below that is Lachha, which includes the red part plus some yellow style, and Zarda or Gucchi, which includes the entire stigma with the full yellow portion. Each step down in grade means less crocin (the colourant), less picrocrocin (the bitter principle), and less safranal (the aroma compound).

Mongra saffron gives 20–30% more colour per gram than Lachha grade. In a Zafrani Pulao or a pot of Kahwa, the difference is immediately visible — a rich golden hue versus a pale yellow tint. In aroma, Mongra releases a deep, honeyed scent that lingers long after cooking; lower grades often smell grassy or of almost nothing at all.

The labour involved is extreme. Each saffron crocus produces just 3 stigmas. After hand-harvesting the flowers at dawn — the only time they're open — workers separate the three red filaments from the yellow style by hand, one flower at a time. One kilogram of Mongra requires roughly 150,000 flowers and over 100 hours of skilled hand work.

When you buy Mongra grade, you're buying the most concentrated, potent form of one of the world's most ancient spices. At Saffron of Kashmir, we source exclusively Mongra grade, tested and certified to ISO 3632 Grade A standards.

The Saffron Fields of Pampore: A 700-Year Legacy

Twenty kilometres south of Srinagar lies Pampore — the "Saffron Town of India" — whose Karewa plateau produces 90% of India's saffron and some of the finest on earth.

Read full article

Saffron cultivation in Kashmir stretches back to at least the 14th century, when Persian traders are believed to have introduced the crocus to the Valley. The Mughal emperors were among the most ardent patrons of Kashmiri saffron — Akbar reportedly ordered saffron planted in royal gardens across the subcontinent, but only the Pampore variety produced the quality he sought. The reason is the soil: the Karewa formation is a rare geological deposit of glacial silt, mineral-rich, with perfect drainage for the moisture-sensitive crocus.

The harvest happens in a dramatic 15-to-20-day window in October and November, when the purple flowers emerge almost overnight. Entire farming families move into the fields before dawn, picking flowers by hand before the sun opens them fully. Delay even an hour, and the petals wilt and the stigmas lose potency — one of the most time-pressured harvests in the world.

By the early 2000s, the industry was in crisis: declining groundwater, erratic snowfall, and competition from cheaper Iranian saffron pushed many farmers to abandon the crop. The area under cultivation fell from 5,700 hectares in the 1990s to under 2,500 by 2010.

Saffron of Kashmir was founded in 2004 precisely to address this — establishing direct partnerships with Pampore farming families at fair prices, giving them the economic incentive to keep growing what the Mughals prized above all spices. Every tin of our saffron represents a direct line from these ancient fields to your kitchen.

How to Test Saffron Purity at Home (3 Methods)

A 2019 study found over 50% of commercially available saffron in the Middle East contained adulterants — dyed corn silk, safflower petals, even coloured paper fibres. Here's how to verify yours.

Read full article

Method 1: The Cold Water Soak

Place 3–4 threads in a glass of cold water and watch for 10–15 minutes. Real saffron slowly releases a deep golden-yellow colour while the thread itself stays crimson. Fake (dyed) saffron bleaches almost immediately, turning pale while the water goes a harsh orange-red.

Method 2: The Baking Soda Test

Add a pinch of baking soda to your saffron water. Real saffron turns the water bright golden-yellow. Adulterated saffron often turns it red, green, or murky brown as the alkaline soda reacts with synthetic pigments.

Method 3: The Rub Test

Rub a single thread between your fingers. Genuine saffron stains your fingers golden-yellow and softens without crumbling. Fake threads bleed colour quickly and disintegrate, often leaving an unnaturally bright orange-red dye.

Note: These are consumer tests. Professional testing uses HPLC spectrophotometry to measure crocin concentration and ISO 3632 compliance. All our saffron is batch-tested to Grade A standards — certificate available on request.

Using Kashmiri Saffron in Arabic Cuisine

Saffron has been part of Arab cuisine for over a thousand years — from celebratory Machboos to saffron-scented Gahwa and the delicate Umm Ali dessert.

Read full article

Machboos (Kabsa): The Gulf's answer to biryani. Use 8–10 threads of Mongra saffron, bloomed for 20 minutes in 3 tablespoons of warm (not boiling) water, then added with rose water in the final steaming stage. The goal is a subtle golden colour and floral perfume — not an aggressive yellow. Full Machboos recipe here.

Saffron Gahwa: Traditional Arabic cardamom coffee with a pinch of saffron added to the grounds. Use just 3–4 threads per pot — saffron is a supporting note here, adding warmth and a faint bittersweet edge.

Saffron Milk (Haleeb Zafaran): Bloom 8–10 threads in a tablespoon of warm milk, then add to a full warm cup with honey, cardamom, and pistachios — the Arabic equivalent of Kesar Doodh, drunk daily across Emirati households.

The golden rule: Always bloom saffron in warm liquid (water, milk, stock — never oil) before adding it to your dish, and never grind Mongra saffron dry — it destroys the delicate aromatic compounds.

Health Benefits of Saffron Backed by Research

Saffron has been used medicinally for over 3,000 years in Persian, Ayurvedic, and Unani traditions. Modern research is catching up.

Read full article

Mood and mental health

A 2014 meta-analysis in Human Psychopharmacology reviewed five randomised controlled trials and found saffron supplementation (30mg/day) significantly more effective than placebo for mild-to-moderate depression. The compound responsible appears to be crocin, which influences serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine pathways.

Antioxidant properties

Saffron contains more than 150 compounds, including crocin, crocetin, and safranal — among the most powerful plant antioxidants identified, able to neutralise reactive oxygen species and reduce lipid peroxidation.

Vision and eye health

Studies from the University of L'Aquila (Italy) found daily saffron supplementation improved vision in patients with age-related macular degeneration. The crocetin in saffron appears to improve oxygen transport to the retina and protect photoreceptor cells.

Dosage note

Research doses range from 20–100mg per day; a generous pinch of Mongra saffron (~30mg) in your daily Kahwa or Kesar Doodh falls within this range. This is not medical advice — consult your healthcare provider for therapeutic applications.
